The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) is making historic investments in America‚Äôs water infrastructure through the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, aiming to upgrade aging systems and address critical water quality issues. 3. Stopping The Use Of Pesticides üìâ

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has taken a major step to protect public health by banning the use of the pesticide chlorpyrifos on all food. Chlorpyrifos, an organophosphate insecticide used on crops like soybeans, fruit and nut trees, and vegetables, has been linked to neurotoxicity and potential neurological effects in children.

In response to a court order and a long-standing petition from environmental and public health groups, the EPA revoked all tolerances for chlorpyrifos, meaning no amount of the pesticide is allowed on food. This decision aligns with actions taken by the European Union, Canada, and several U.S. states, including California, Hawaii, and New York, to restrict its use.

While farmers have historically relied on chlorpyrifos, its use has already been declining due to state-level restrictions and the availability of safer alternatives. By following the science and prioritizing public health, the EPA's ban on chlorpyrifos ensures safer food and better protection for children and farmworkers from harmful pesticide exposure.

4. Reducing Led Exposure üìâ

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has developed the Strategy to Reduce Lead Exposures and Disparities in U.S. Communities to tackle the critical issue of lead exposure, especially in high-risk communities. This strategy focuses on eliminating disparities in blood lead levels that disproportionately affect children in racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic communities.

The EPA's approach involves four key goals: reducing community exposures to lead sources, identifying high-exposure areas, improving communication with stakeholders, and supporting research to inform policy decisions.

The strategy has three main approaches: targeting local communities with a focus on environmental justice, developing national standards and tools to reduce lead exposures, and collaborating with various federal, state, tribal, and local partners to address lead contamination on a broader scale.

Through extensive public engagement and feedback, the EPA has refined the strategy to ensure effective, evidence-based actions that will protect children‚Äôs health and promote equity. This initiative underscores the agency‚Äôs commitment to reducing lead exposure risks, improving health outcomes, and advancing environmental justice across the United States.
